# A Commons Legislative Program for the United States

**Four-Bill Series | Citizen-Drafted Federal Legislation**

---

## Overview

This repository contains four independently operable but architecturally interdependent federal bills, drafted in standard legislative format and ready for committee referral. Together they constitute a complete transition architecture for scaling the commons economy from the margins of American life to its institutional foundation.

The program is grounded in the empirical governance research of Nobel laureate **Elinor Ostrom**, whose eight commons governance design principles are encoded throughout as operative legal standards rather than aspirational language. It treats governance as a systems engineering problem: the question is not who should hold power in the abstract, but what institutional architecture produces durable, self-correcting, community-governed resource systems in practice.

The bills are designed for **staggered introduction**, not simultaneous release. Each bill builds the legal, material, and political infrastructure on which the next one depends.

## The Bills

### Vol. I: Federal Resilience and Decentralization Initiatives Act (FRDI Act)

**The material commons. Introduce first.**

The FRDI Act is the foundational instrument. It establishes the legal, technical, and financial infrastructure on which the entire program operates:

- A national network of **Community Land Trusts (CLTs)** holding land in permanent community stewardship, insulated from speculation and financialization
- **Worker and consumer cooperative** formation pathways, succession rights, and conversion mechanisms, including a Right of First Refusal for employee buyouts of businesses facing closure or sale
- A **Federated Data Commons** with data sovereignty protections, surveillance prohibition, and cryptographic privacy architecture
- An **Intellectual Property Commons** including reversal of the Bayh-Dole Act's privatization of publicly funded research, voluntary dedication pathways for creators, and an abandoned-works doctrine
- A **Commons Credit System** (Stewardship Credits) recognizing productive land stewardship without creating dollar-denominated repayment obligations
- The **Federated Mesh Open Standard (FMOS)** and **Asynchronous Resource Coordination (ARC)** protocol: open-source technical infrastructure connecting commons nodes horizontally without centralized administration
- **Federated Commons Membership**: a bundle of democratic stewardship rights in cooperative institutions providing housing, nutrition, healthcare, connectivity, and knowledge access

The FRDI Act must be introduced first. It is the material foundation that must be operational before the punitive systems addressed in Vol. II can be safely dismantled, and the resilience infrastructure that must be demonstrated before the defense posture reforms of Vol. III can be credibly advanced.

---

### Vol. II: Human Reclamation and Community Restorative Act (HRCR Act)

**The social commons. Introduce concurrent with or shortly after Vol. I.**

The HRCR Act replaces the punitive carceral system with community-governed restorative processes, using the material infrastructure established by the FRDI Act as its foundation:

- **Accountability Pods and Harm Repair Agreements**: localized, peer-governed restorative processes replacing incarceration as the primary response to harm
- **Decarceration architecture**: sentence review mechanisms, compassionate release expansion, and population reduction timelines for federal correctional facilities
- **Conversion of federal correctional facilities** to Commons Resilience Campuses, repurposing existing physical infrastructure for community benefit
- **Abolition of extractive monetary assessments** in federal criminal proceedings: fines, fees, and court costs that function as debt traps rather than accountability mechanisms
- **Demilitarization of domestic policing** through repeal of the 1033 Program and prohibition on transfer of military equipment to civilian law enforcement
- **Immediate, unconditional commons membership** for every person completing a restorative process, removing the material precarity that the punitive system's coercive leverage depends on

The HRCR Act is architecturally dependent on the FRDI Act. A restorative justice system without a material floor produces the same desperation that drives people back into punitive loops. The two bills together are the demonstration that material security and community accountability are the same project.

---

### Vol. III: Federal Commons Security and Automation Governance Act (FCSAG)

**The strategic commons. Introduce two to three years after Vol. I enactment.**

The FCSAG reorients national defense and technology policy toward resilience-based security, and addresses the defining economic question of the coming decades: who captures the productivity gains that automation generates.

- **Resilience-based defense posture**: reorienting from overseas power projection toward distributed domestic infrastructure security, treating the federated commons network as a civilian resilience capacity
- **Open-source analytics commons**: capturing publicly funded intelligence analytics platforms, stripping surveillance capabilities, and converting them to open-source coordination tools for commons resource management
- **Automation Dividend**: a legal mechanism ensuring that productivity gains from automated systems deployed within the commons network flow to commons members through reduced labor requirements and improved provision standards, rather than to private capital
- **Algorithmic Governance Prohibition**: prohibiting automated final determinations affecting individual rights, anchored in the Fifth Amendment's due process guarantee
- **Energy commons provisions**: distributed renewable energy infrastructure governed by QFN stewardship standards, reducing dependence on centralized fossil fuel supply chains

The FCSAG is introduced after the FRDI Act has demonstrated its resilience value. Introduced prematurely, it faces concentrated national security opposition without the demonstrated domestic infrastructure to answer the fragility critique. Introduced after the commons network is operational, it is responsible strategic adaptation.

---

### Vol. IV: International Sovereignty, Resilience and Nonproliferation Act (ISRN)

**The global commons. Introduce last.**

The ISRN governs the outward-facing dimension of the transition: the renegotiation of alliance relationships, the reform of multilateral institutions, and the legal architecture for a planetary commons network.

- **NATO resilience framework**: governance provisions for the ongoing Europeanization of NATO, reorienting the alliance from American power projection toward distributed allied resilience
- **Nuclear posture reform**: minimum credible deterrence doctrine, multilateral disarmament commitments, and prohibition on first-use deployment
- **Multilateral institution reform**: structural reform of the IMF and World Bank to eliminate structural adjustment conditions that have prevented commons development in the Global South
- **International Commons Federation Agreements**: legal architecture for connecting domestic Qualified Federated Networks with commons institutions internationally, building a planetary mesh without center or hierarchy
- **Technology export controls**: prohibition on the export of carceral technology, mass surveillance software, and predictive policing systems to foreign governments

The ISRN is introduced last because its arguments are most credible when the domestic commons transition has demonstrated its viability. A nation proposing resilience-based alliance governance to its partners is most persuasive when it can point to a functioning domestic resilience infrastructure as proof of concept.

## Governance as Systems Engineering

The dominant failure mode of political reform is ideological: defining the desired end state in advance and building institutions to enforce it. This produces fragile systems that depend on continuous coercive maintenance and collapse when the political conditions that created them shift.

This program takes a different approach. It treats governance as a systems engineering problem. The question is not what the correct political outcome is, but what institutional architecture produces communities capable of governing their own shared resources durably, adaptively, and without dependence on either a corporate landlord or a state bureaucrat.

Elinor Ostrom spent her career answering that question empirically. Her eight design principles are not a political platform. They are a specification derived from analysis of commons institutions across centuries and cultures, identifying the features that distinguished those that thrived from those that failed. This program encodes those specifications as operative legal standards: the **Ostrom Standards of Stewardship**, which every Qualified Federated Network must meet to receive certification and access to commons infrastructure.

The practical consequences of this approach:

**No panaceas.** Ostrom's most important methodological principle was the rejection of universal solutions. The program does not impose a single governance model. It establishes design criteria and lets communities build institutions that meet them in ways appropriate to their specific social, ecological, and economic conditions. A rural agricultural cooperative and an urban housing commons and an open-source software collective can all be Qualified Federated Networks. They will look different. They will govern differently. They share a common architecture.

**Polycentric coordination.** The program is built around multiple, overlapping, autonomous governance centers that coordinate horizontally rather than reporting to a central authority. The FMOS and ARC protocols are the technical expression of this: a coordination standard that routes resources and information peer-to-peer without requiring central planning or corporate market intermediation.

**Self-correction over enforcement.** Ostrom's design principles emphasize graduated sanctions, fast low-cost conflict resolution, and collective rule modification. The program builds these into the governance architecture of every certified commons institution rather than relying on federal administrative machinery to maintain compliance.

**Subsidiarity as operative standard.** Governance authority is located at the smallest scale capable of effectively addressing the matter at issue. The program reduces federal administrative reach not to create a vacuum, but to fill it with community-governed institutions operating under verified design criteria.

This is why the program resists easy ideological classification. It is not a state-expansion program: it systematically reduces federal administrative reach while increasing community self-determination. It is not a privatization program: it removes resources from both state administration and private enclosure and places them under democratic community stewardship. It addresses the failure modes of both dominant institutional options without adopting either's logic.

## The Proof of Concept

The program is not theoretical. Every major institutional form it proposes to scale already exists and already functions:

| Institution | Existing Proof of Concept |
|---|---|
| Community Development Finance | CDFI network ($222B+ in assets, 1,400+ certified institutions nationally) |
| Community Land Trust | Champlain Housing Trust, Burlington VT (2,000+ permanently affordable homes) |
| Worker Cooperative Federation | Mondragon, Basque Country (80,000+ worker-owners, 100+ cooperatives) |
| Participatory Governance Technology | Decidim platform (Barcelona and hundreds of cities globally) |
| Open-Source Knowledge Commons | Linux, Wikipedia (global scale, exceeding proprietary alternatives in value) |
| Solidarity Economy Networks | XES (Catalonia), New Economy Coalition (200+ US member organizations) |
| Time-Banking Infrastructure | TimeBanks USA (hundreds of active networks) |

The FRDI Act does not create these institutions. It removes the legal barriers that keep them fragmented, provides the technical infrastructure that connects them, and supplies the financial capitalization that allows them to scale. The answer to the charge of utopianism is empirical: this already exists. The program builds roads between cities that already stand.

## Elinor Ostrom's Eight Design Principles

Every Qualified Federated Network certified under this program must demonstrate compliance with all eight principles, codified as the **Ostrom Standards of Stewardship**:

1. **Clearly Defined Boundaries**: the resource pool and the community of users are precisely defined
2. **Congruence with Local Conditions**: governance rules match the ecological and social realities of the specific commons
3. **Collective-Choice Arrangements**: those governed by the rules have meaningful participation in modifying them
4. **Accountable Monitoring**: resource condition and user behavior are monitored by users or those directly accountable to them
5. **Graduated Sanctions**: rule violations are addressed through proportionate, escalating responses rather than immediate punitive action
6. **Fast, Low-Cost Conflict Resolution**: disputes are resolved through accessible local mechanisms rather than costly external adjudication
7. **Minimal Recognition of Rights to Organize**: the community's right to build its own governance institutions is not challenged by external authorities
8. **Nested Enterprises**: for complex systems, governance is organized in layered, polycentric tiers allowing local autonomy within larger coordinating networks

**"No panaceas."** Ostrom's guiding methodological principle: no single governance model works for every resource, every community, or every scale. The program embeds this principle in its design by requiring compliance with the eight criteria while leaving the specific institutional form to community determination.

## Constitutional Foundations

The program is designed to be read as constitutionally grounded from multiple interpretive frameworks simultaneously. Each bill's Findings section states its constitutional anchors explicitly.

- **FRDI Act**: Article I Section 8 (IP Commons, promotion of useful arts for limited times; the public funded it, the public should have it); Tenth Amendment (decentralization, subsidiarity, jurisdictional autonomy)
- **HRCR Act**: Eighth Amendment (prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment); Thirteenth Amendment (involuntary servitude); Fourteenth Amendment (equal protection, due process)
- **FCSAG**: Fifth Amendment (due process, prohibition on automated final determinations affecting individual rights); Article I Section 8 (defense and general welfare)
- **ISRN**: Article II Section 2 (treaty power); Article I Section 8 (commerce, defense); Supremacy Clause

## How to Use This Repository

**Legislators and legislative staff**: these bills are ready for committee referral. They are drafted in standard federal legislative format with cross-references, conforming amendments, and administrative provisions. The Findings sections of each bill provide the evidentiary record that a Committee Report would ordinarily supply. The staggered introduction strategy means that Vol. I can be introduced and advanced independently while the remaining volumes are reviewed.

**Advocates and legal organizations**: each bill's provisions are independently citable and can be used as model language for state-level legislation, municipal ordinances, or organizational governance documents.

**Practitioners and community builders**: the governance architecture specified in the FRDI Act, particularly the Qualified Federated Network certification criteria and the FMOS/ARC technical standards, is designed to be implemented at the community level in advance of federal enactment. The Constitutional Safe Harbor provisions in each bill are designed to protect institutions built in conformity with the program's standards.

**Researchers and theorists**: the Findings sections of each bill constitute a substantive engagement with the empirical literature on commons governance, cooperative economics, restorative justice, and distributed security. Citations throughout reference Ostrom's design principles, CLT models, cooperative federation structures, and related scholarship.
